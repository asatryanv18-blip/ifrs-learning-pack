Section 01 — Overview

Why IFRS 16 exists and what it changed

Before IFRS 16, companies could structure lease agreements to keep significant assets and liabilities off the balance sheet — a practice that distorted financial ratios and made companies look less leveraged than they truly were. IFRS 16 ended this by requiring lessees to recognize almost all leases on the balance sheet.

Core objective

IFRS 16 requires lessees to recognize a right-of-use (ROU) asset and a corresponding lease liability for all leases with a term exceeding 12 months, unless the underlying asset is of low value.

What changed from IAS 17

IAS 17 (old)
Two-model approach: finance leases on balance sheet, operating leases kept off balance sheet. Companies exploited this to hide leverage.
IFRS 16 (current)
Single lessee model: virtually all leases go on balance sheet. The lessor accounting model is broadly unchanged from IAS 17.
Impact on lessee

Higher total assets, higher total liabilities, lower operating costs (replaced by depreciation + interest), improved EBITDA, lower net income in early years.

Impact on ratios

Debt-to-equity increases. Interest coverage decreases. Asset turnover decreases. EBITDA margin improves (lease payments no longer in EBIT).

Key definitions

TermDefinition
LeaseA contract that conveys the right to control the use of an identified asset for a period of time in exchange for consideration.
Right-of-use assetAn asset representing the lessee's right to use an underlying asset for the lease term.
Lease liabilityThe present value of lease payments not yet made, discounted at the interest rate implicit in the lease or the lessee's incremental borrowing rate.
Lease termThe non-cancellable period plus optional extension periods the lessee is reasonably certain to exercise, plus optional termination periods the lessee is reasonably certain NOT to exercise.
Incremental borrowing rate (IBR)The rate of interest that a lessee would have to pay to borrow the funds necessary to obtain an asset of similar value in a similar economic environment.
Commencement dateThe date on which a lessor makes an underlying asset available for use by a lessee.
Section 02 — Recognition & Measurement

Initial and subsequent measurement

Two exemptions — no balance sheet recognition required

Short-term leases — lease term of 12 months or less at commencement date.
Low-value assets — when the underlying asset is of low value (IASB guidance: approximately USD 5,000 when new). Examples: laptops, tablets, small office furniture.

Initial recognition — lease liability

At the commencement date, measure the lease liability at the present value of the lease payments not yet made, discounted using the interest rate implicit in the lease (or IBR if that rate cannot be readily determined).

Lease payments included in the measurement:

  • Fixed payments (less any lease incentives receivable)
  • Variable payments that depend on an index or rate
  • Exercise price of a purchase option (if reasonably certain to exercise)
  • Penalties for terminating (if the lease term reflects lessee exercising a termination option)
  • Amounts expected to be payable under residual value guarantees

Initial recognition — ROU asset

The ROU asset is initially measured at cost, which comprises:

Subsequent measurement

Lease liability (after commencement)

Increase by interest accrued each period (unwinding of discount). Decrease by lease payments made. Remeasure when there is a lease modification, change in assessment of extension/termination options, or change in index/rate.

ROU asset (after commencement)

Depreciate over the shorter of the lease term and the useful life of the underlying asset (unless transfer of ownership or purchase option exercise is likely — then depreciate over useful life). Test for impairment under IAS 36.

Common exam trap

In early years of a lease, the lease liability reduces more slowly than the ROU asset depreciates (because a large portion of early payments goes to interest). This means the carrying amount of the lease liability can exceed the ROU asset in the early years — this is normal and correct.

How to read the schedule

The opening lease liability at Year 1 equals the initial PV of lease payments. Each year, the liability grows by the interest charge (unwinding of discount) and shrinks by the cash payment. The difference is the principal reduction. By the final year, the closing balance should be zero (or very close, due to rounding).

Under IAS 17 (old): Operating lease expense = $50,000 per year — straight line, entirely in operating expenses.

Audit focus area

In practice, auditors focus on: (1) completeness of the lease population — have all leases been captured? (2) accuracy of the IBR — is it a reasonable estimate of the lessee's actual borrowing cost? (3) lease term assessment — has management appropriately included/excluded extension options? These three areas drive the vast majority of audit differences.

Presentation in financial statements

Statement of financial position

ROU assets: either presented separately or within the same line item as the owned assets of the same class (disclose which approach).

Lease liabilities: either separately or within borrowings. Current / non-current split required.

Cash flow statement

Principal repayments of lease liability → financing activities.
Interest paid on lease liability → operating or financing activities (accounting policy choice, IAS 7).
Short-term and low-value payments → operating activities.

Section 06 — Exam Practice

Test your knowledge

Eight questions covering the most frequently examined areas of IFRS 16. Click an answer to reveal the explanation.

Question 01
On 1 January 20X1, Entity A enters a 4-year lease for office space with annual payments of $30,000 payable in arrears. The rate implicit in the lease cannot be determined; Entity A's IBR is 5%. What is the initial lease liability?
Correct. The lease liability = PV of an ordinary annuity of $30,000 for 4 years at 5% = $30,000 × [(1 − 1.05⁻⁴) / 0.05] = $30,000 × 3.5460 = $106,382. Answer A ($120,000) is the undiscounted total — a common trap.
Question 02
Which of the following is NOT included in the initial measurement of the lease liability under IFRS 16?
Correct. Variable lease payments that do NOT depend on an index or rate (e.g. payments linked to sales or usage) are excluded from the lease liability. They are expensed in the period in which the condition triggering the payment occurs (IFRS 16.38).
Question 03
Entity B has a 3-year lease with a 2-year extension option. At commencement, management considers it NOT reasonably certain to exercise the option. One year later, circumstances change and management now considers it reasonably certain. How should Entity B account for this?
Correct. A change in the assessment of an extension option triggers remeasurement of the lease liability (IFRS 16.45). The revised discount rate is the IBR at the reassessment date. The corresponding adjustment goes to the ROU asset — not to P&L.
Question 04
Under IFRS 16, how should a lessee classify lease payments in the cash flow statement?
Correct. The principal portion of lease payments (repayment of the lease liability) must be classified as financing activities. The interest portion may be classified as operating or financing activities — this is an accounting policy choice under IAS 7, applied consistently.
Question 05
An entity leases a machine for 8 years. The machine has a useful economic life of 6 years. The lease does not transfer ownership and there is no purchase option. Over what period should the ROU asset be depreciated?
Correct. IFRS 16.31 states that if ownership does NOT transfer and there is no purchase option the lessee is reasonably certain to exercise, the ROU asset is depreciated over the shorter of the lease term and the useful life of the underlying asset. Here: shorter of 8 and 6 = 6 years.
Question 06
Which of the following leases qualifies for the short-term lease practical expedient under IFRS 16?
Correct. The short-term exemption applies to leases with a term of 12 months or less at commencement. A 10-month lease qualifies. Option A does not qualify because the lease term is 14 months (since exercise of the termination option is not reasonably certain). Option D does not qualify because a lease containing a purchase option cannot use the short-term exemption.
Question 07
In the early years of a lease, compared to the old operating lease treatment under IAS 17, IFRS 16 will result in:
Correct. In early years: depreciation + interest exceeds the straight-line operating lease expense (front-loaded profile), so total expense is higher and profit is lower. EBITDA is higher because the old lease expense was above EBIT, whereas under IFRS 16, depreciation is included in EBIT but interest (finance cost) is excluded from EBITDA. Net effect: EBITDA improves.
Question 08
Entity C subleases a right-of-use asset to Entity D. How should Entity C classify this sublease?
Correct. IFRS 16.B58 requires that when classifying a sublease, an intermediate lessor classifies the sublease by reference to the ROU asset arising from the head lease — not by reference to the underlying asset. This is a key difference from general lessor accounting under IFRS 16 (which uses the underlying asset as the reference).
